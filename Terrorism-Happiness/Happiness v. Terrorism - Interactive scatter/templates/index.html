Happiness vs. Everything

What makes a country happy? Or unhappy?

We are not just individuals, and an individual is not just a thing in itself. Individuals are products of their environments and parts of a society. For that reason, we can look to societal trends and relate them to individual happiness, or unhappiness. In this page, we'll look at which countries have the happiest individuals and (along with terrorism) other trends that may positively or negatively correlate with said happiness.

Life expectancy

A country's life expectancy is the highest correlated factor with happiness index score. This makes sense as people who tend to live long have good access to food, healthcare, and financial security. That means less worries and more time to devote to things that make them happy. In the chart, you can see the Scandinavian social democracies such as Denmark and Finland have the highest happiness scores.

Caveat here, along with all other factors, is that variance among individuals in a country has not been tracked. A country can still have the happiest individuals in the world along with the unhappiest, but our data doesn't contain that information.

GDP
Coming in a close second, a country's GDP also has a strong correlation with happiness. GDP measures a country's economic activity and represents which countries have the highest value goods and may suggest which countries have the most disposable income. So money does buy us happiness.

Maybe not all the happiness within us, but financial security and cool things do give us quite a bit.

Alcoholism Rate
I'd expect alcoholism to have a negative correlation with happiness, but it actually has an insignficant positive correlation with a p-value of 0.1. Many of the most alcoholic countries tend to be Eastern European and former Soviet countries with average happiness scores. Russia isn't on the scatter plot below, but it would fall in line with these countries.

Maybe legitimate drinking cultures accidentally produce alcohlics just by sheer volume of heavy drinkers.

Suicide Rate
This trend here makes some sense. Suicide rate has a negative correlation with happiness. The weird part, though, is that it's an insignificant correlation (p-value = -0.14). There can be many explanations for this. For example, major variance of happiness can exist within individual countries due to social and financial inequalities. We don't have the data here, but it's probably true.

Terrorist Act Rate
And here's another trend that makes some sense, but not completely. Terrorist acts per population has a negative correlation with happiness, but like suicide rate, to an insiginficant degree (p-value = -0.13).

Most countries' terrorist rates are close to zero anyway. The more interesting trend is the top 3 most terrorized countries (Iraq, Afghanistan, and Libya) are all countries suffering from recent US intervention.

Operation Iraqi freedom is still operating, right?

Drug Abuse
Now this is where stuff gets weird. Drug abuse has a strong positive correlation with a p-value of 0.58. And America is #1. We're number 1! We're number 1!

USA! USA! USA!

Anxiety
Anxiety somehow also has a strong correlation with happiness. When it comes to drug abuse and anxiety, I hypothesize that many of those suffering from them probably weren't surveyed for the happiness report.

Even in lands of opportunity, there can be many people who feel alienated due to financial inequalities, not living up to certain cultural values, and our overemphasis on the individual over the collective. But the topic of loneliness, culture, and financial insecurity are subjects for another time.
